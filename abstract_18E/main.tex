\section{Introduction}
The DOE national laboratory system maintains some of the most complex and consequential scientific software in the world~\cite{perabytes}, encoding decades of expertise in numerical methods, simulation algorithms, and HPC architectures across facilities such as Argonne National Laboratory, Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and Sandia National Laboratories. These codebases must evolve continuously---porting to new GPU architectures, adapting to new hardware constraints, incorporating new numerical methods---while preserving the correctness and reproducibility that scientific integrity demands~\cite{keahey2025report,pouchard2019computational}. This widening \textbf{software trustworthiness gap} poses significant challenges for national research priorities: the complexity of HPC scientific software outpaces the capacity of human reviewers to manually verify every AI-assisted change, creating critical accountability risk for DOE's scientific mission.

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) and generative AI have produced tools of remarkable capability to assist with scientific software development. AI-based systems can suggest code optimizations~\cite{podduturi2025ai}, identify performance bottlenecks~\cite{polu2025ai}, and translate legacy codebases at significant scale~\cite{claude}. The potential of LLM-based agents to accelerate HPC software development is widely recognized and actively being pursued across the national laboratory system~\cite{rummler2026sandians}. However, LLMs are prone to hallucinate---generating plausible-sounding recommendations that conflate numerically distinct algorithms, suggest transformations that do not preserve correctness, or cite validation results that do not support the proposed optimization~\cite{Ji2023HallucinationSurvey}. These outcomes can corrupt production scientific software and invalidate research results. Recently, agentic systems coordinating multiple AI agents~\cite{ezzrhari2020scalable} and augmentation methods to retrieve relevant documentation~\cite{miyashita2024llm} aim to ground code recommendations in existing knowledge. However, fundamental challenges that constrain AI utility for trustworthy HPC software development remain:

\textbf{Absence of persistent knowledge state.} Current AI systems treat each interaction independently, with no persistent representation of a codebase's development history, performance characteristics, or the rationale behind prior decisions. This prevents AI from reasoning about accumulated software knowledge, detecting regressions against established benchmarks, or avoiding repeated analysis of validated transformations.

\textbf{Lack of traceable provenance.} Trustworthy software engineering requires that decisions be justifiable and reproducible. When an AI system recommends a code transformation, developers must be able to trace that recommendation to specific benchmarks, numerical analyses, or validation results. Current systems may surface relevant documentation but do not provide structured reasoning that connects evidence to recommendations---making AI-assisted code changes impossible to audit or reproduce independently. \vspace{-5pt}


\problem{The lack of structured and persistent knowledge about HPC software development limits AI agents' ability to provide trustworthy, reproducible assistance for DOE scientific software.}

To address these challenges, this project proposes the design, implementation, and evaluation of \textbf{provenance-aware agentic knowledge graphs} (PA-AKG) for HPC scientific software development. PA-AKG is foundational infrastructure for trustworthy, AI-integrated software engineering, addressing the challenges above by providing AI agents with a persistent, structured, and auditable representation of the current state of a scientific software system in a given domain. Knowledge graphs (KGs) have been applied in software engineering and scientific computing as a mechanism for representing complex, heterogeneous information through explicit semantic relationships~\cite{karras2024organizing,kousha2023democratizing}. PA-AKG will encode HPC software knowledge (\ie algorithms, numerical methods, performance benchmarks, hardware constraints, validation results, and development decisions) as nodes representing existing knowledge, with relationships between them as directed edges. We extend these efforts by embedding agentic orchestration. As AI agents suggest code transformations, they compare proposed changes against KG-encoded performance expectations. When decisions are confirmed, they are written back to the KG with explicit provenance chains---carrying the specific benchmarks and evidence that justify each change---accumulating an auditable development history that future agents can build upon.

\section{Project Objectives} 

Our goal is to design and implement a provenance-first agentic KG architecture with three integrated layers: knowledge representation; automation and extraction; and agentic orchestration. We will implement the PA-AKG architecture, developing infrastructure to automate the extraction and structuring of claim-evidence pairs in HPC software contexts with role-specific agents (\eg ranking agents, evaluation agents, and synthesis agents). The initial implementation will focus on the software engineering research domain in the context of HPC, following our prior work~\cite{cusati2026fose}, with secondary validation against HPC performance analysis workflows at Sandia National Laboratories. Software engineering outlines processes and methods for developing and maintaining software systems, and is also critical for technological innovation~\cite{vos2026reclaiming} and enabling the development and maintenance of applications~\cite{schmidberger2012need}. We will conduct a comprehensive evaluation of our approach, comparing PA-AKG against RAG, GraphRAG~\cite{Edge2024GraphRAG}, and static baseline approaches on provenance completeness, reproducibility, and trustworthiness of AI-generated software decisions. We will also conduct controlled user studies with HPC software developers to assess usability and utility. \section{Proposed Research and Methods} 

Our research plan involves four main phases:

\paragraph{Phase 1 (Months 1--3): Knowledge Representation Layer.}
We will design the HPC software KG schema with entity types such as \textsc{Algorithm}, \textsc{NumericalMethod}, \textsc{CodeComponent}, \textsc{HardwareConstraint}, \textsc{Benchmark}, and \textsc{ValidationResult}---linked by semantic relations (\textsc{implements}, \textsc{requires}, \textsc{supersedes}, \textsc{validates}). We will conduct pilot studies (\eg interviews with HPC software developers) to obtain insights from practitioners, informing entity types and relationships for our initial implementation. A hybrid symbolic--semantic design pairs a property graph store with a vector index, supporting both structured relational queries and approximate similarity search. We will populate an initial KG from the software engineering research corpus established in prior work, validate entity extraction against manually annotated ground-truth annotations, and establish baseline KG coverage metrics.

\paragraph{Phase 2 (Months 4--6): Automation, Extraction, and Agentic Orchestration.}
We will implement the extraction and automation pipeline: structured prompting will produce claim--evidence pairs; span-level alignment will link each claim to character-level source passage spans; and canonicalization auditing will normalize, validate, and deduplicate entity references to canonical KG nodes. The agentic orchestration layer, adapting the Planning \& Control pattern~\cite{Park2023GenerativeAgents}, introduces four role-specific agent types. \textit{Ranking agents} score candidate code changes by evidence strength and risk. \textit{Continuation agents} propose follow-on validation analyses grounded in KG gaps. \textit{Evaluation agents} execute reproducible benchmark workflows and write results back to the KG as structured evidence nodes. \textit{Synthesis agents} generate recommendation artifacts---proposed transformation, supporting evidence chain, predicted performance impact---with full provenance metadata. The provenance inheritance constraint is enforced at every agent handoff, ensuring no reasoning step discards the justification for prior decisions.

\paragraph{Phase 3 (Months 7--9): Evaluation and Generalization.}
We will conduct two comprehensive evaluations of our PA-AKG approach. The first will benchmark our system against existing approaches, including vector-based RAG, hybrid retrieval, and GraphRAG-style reasoning. We will construct a benchmark of human-annotated claim--evidence pairs from the SE research corpus. We will calculate span-level alignment precision/recall, Cohen's $\kappa$ with expert-labeled canonical entity clusters, and traceability completeness (\ie \% of KG nodes with complete provenance chains). A secondary HPC validation at Sandia National Laboratories will assess cross-domain generalizability of the extraction pipeline. Our second evaluation will assess provenance completeness and trustworthiness of AI-assisted code recommendations through controlled user studies with HPC software practitioners, collecting quantitative task-completion metrics and qualitative feedback on recommendation auditability.

\paragraph{Phase 4: Dissemination.}
We will pursue targeted dissemination of PA-AKG research findings to academic and practitioner audiences. Academic outputs will be submitted to premier venues in software engineering, AI, and machine learning, including the International Conference on Software Engineering (ICSE), the Mining Software Repositories conference (MSR), and the Supercomputing Conference (SC). We will submit to tool, demo, and tutorial tracks at these venues to provide hands-on exposure of PA-AKG to HPC software development and software engineering research communities, lowering the barrier to adoption across DOE and academic research programs. To reach the practitioner community, we will release the PA-AKG implementation as an open-source package with comprehensive documentation, worked examples, and integration guides targeting national laboratory developers and the broader scientific computing community. We will present findings at Sandia National Laboratories and partner institutions to gather practitioner feedback, surface deployment barriers, and assess adoption pathways within active DOE HPC workflows. This phase will conclude with an impact assessment evaluating how PA-AKG changes developers' ability to audit and reproduce AI-assisted code decisions---informing the design of future trustworthy AI systems for HPC scientific software and establishing PA-AKG as a reference model for accountable AI assistance in DOE's software engineering mission.
